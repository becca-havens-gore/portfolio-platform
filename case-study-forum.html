Turning Slack Threads Into Structured Design Decisions That Actually Scale

The Problem: Decisions Made in the Wrong Places

As Rapid7's design organization scaled across six product teams, Platform UX, design systems, visual design, and UX research, quality diverged rapidly. There was no shared mechanism for making platform-level design decisions.

Important choices — which navigation pattern to adopt, whether a new data visualization type was acceptable, how to handle a cross-product accessibility edge case — were resolved in Slack threads, in individual designer judgment calls, or not at all. There was no record, no rationale, no way for teams to build on each other's decisions.

What Was Missing

  • No formal review process for new UX patterns
  • No governance model tied to evidence or standards
  • No decision log — rationale was lost after conversations ended
  • No accessibility baseline enforced across products
  • No process for cross-product interaction alignment

What It Caused

  • Design drift: each team's patterns diverged over time
  • Duplicated design work: teams solved the same problems independently
  • Accessibility regression: gaps compounded across releases
  • Engineer uncertainty: "what does compliant look like?" had no answer
  • Increased release risk: UX inconsistencies found late in cycles

"I'm not sure who approves this pattern. I've asked three people and gotten three different answers."

— Designer, pre-Design Forum research interview

Why This Matters for Platform Teams

Developer platform organizations at scale face an identical governance challenge. When teams building CI/CD, observability, IDE integrations, and deployment surfaces each make pattern decisions independently, the platform becomes incoherent even when every individual team is doing good work.

The Design Forum model — a structured, cross-functional decision body with evidence-based evaluation criteria and a transparent decision log — is directly applicable to any platform organization that needs to scale design quality without centralizing all design authority. It's how you keep many teams moving fast while building one coherent experience.

The Framework: Three Interlocking Systems

System 1 — The Design Forum

I designed and launched the Design Forum as a recurring, structured decision-making body for platform-level UX patterns. It replaced informal, distributed pattern approvals with a process that was transparent, cross-functional, and evidence-based.

  • Format: 30-minute weekly sessions with cross-disciplinary attendance (design, engineering, product, accessibility)
  • Submission template: Standardized proposal format covering problem statement, alternatives considered, research evidence, accessibility implications, and technical feasibility
  • Decision log: All decisions recorded in Confluence with rationale, alternatives rejected, and follow-up actions
  • Async transparency: Weekly summaries to #ux-all so the full org had visibility into decisions without attending every session
  • RDS lifecycle integration: Design Forum approval was a required gate before any new pattern entered the design system

System 2 — The FOG Evaluation Model

I introduced a structured evaluation framework to ensure decisions were grounded in evidence rather than seniority or volume:

F
Facts
User research, analytics, accessibility requirements, technical constraints, existing standards. Non-negotiable inputs to any decision.
O
Opinions
Design expertise, cross-team preferences, aesthetic judgment. Valid inputs — but explicitly labeled as opinions so they're weighed appropriately.
G
Guesses
Hypotheses that need testing. Decisions based heavily on Guesses are approved conditionally with a defined validation plan.

The FOG model made power dynamics visible. A strongly-held Opinion from a senior leader carried less weight than a Fact from a user research study. This shifted the culture from "who says" to "what evidence says."

System 3 — UX Standards Framework

I built a four-pillar standards framework that defined what "quality" meant for every design decision:

Pillar 01
Accessibility
WCAG 2.2 AA as the baseline. Defined specific requirements for keyboard navigation, screen reader compatibility, color contrast, and focus management. Integrated into the RDS component spec and Design Forum checklist.
Pillar 02
Responsiveness
Grid, breakpoint, and layout rules applicable across all product surfaces. Defined the minimum responsive behavior for every component and page template.
Pillar 03
Usability
Predictable interaction patterns, cognitive load requirements, error handling standards. Defined what "usable" meant concretely, so it wasn't an opinion in design reviews.
Pillar 04
Governance & Continuous Improvement
Audit cadence, exception process, pattern lifecycle management. Standards without a maintenance process become stale. This pillar ensured the framework evolved.

What Changed the Direction

The Design Forum was adopted faster than expected not because of the process itself, but because of what it replaced. The thing teams hated wasn't the lack of process — it was the unpredictability. Not knowing when a pattern would get reviewed, by whom, with what criteria.

Once we had a predictable weekly cadence, a submission template that set clear expectations, and a decision log that showed prior reasoning, teams began pre-checking their work against existing decisions before submitting. The forum was doing governance work in advance, not just at review time. The process made the standard ambient rather than episodic.

"Now I know what good looks like before I start designing. I can reference a real decision with documented rationale instead of guessing."

— Product designer, post-forum feedback survey

The Tradeoff We Accepted

What We Accepted

We built a 30-minute weekly gate into every team's pattern adoption workflow. This added calendar overhead and occasionally slowed down teams who wanted to ship a new interaction pattern quickly. Some teams initially experienced the forum as friction rather than service.

Why

The alternative cost was higher: patterns shipped without review that conflicted with existing standards required retroactive remediation, often in production. A 30-minute structured review is dramatically cheaper than a post-ship accessibility remediation or a design drift correction that affects six product teams. We were trading short-term friction for long-term quality velocity.
