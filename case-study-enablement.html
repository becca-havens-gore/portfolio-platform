Case Study — Design Enablement & Platform Maturity

How I Scaled Systems Thinking Across a Growing Platform Design Organization

A multi-year program that reduced designer onboarding time 30–40%, built platform-level systems fluency across 30+ designers, and pioneered AI-assisted design education tooling before it was mainstream.

The Problem: Scale Without Foundation

As Rapid7's platform grew in complexity and the design organization expanded to serve it, a gap opened between what the platform demanded and what designers could navigate independently. New designers joining product teams needed weeks to understand platform architecture, system constraints, and decision precedents before they could contribute at full effectiveness.

Experienced designers were informally carrying this knowledge, but the transfer was uneven and exhausting. Design quality depended on who you happened to work near, not on any structural foundation.

What Research Showed

  • New designers averaged 6–8 weeks before contributing effectively to platform-level work
  • Mentorship was entirely informal — no structure, no accountability, no consistency
  • PMs and engineers couldn't engage with design decisions without shared vocabulary
  • AI tools were being explored without a responsible use framework
  • Systems thinking skills were distributed unevenly across seniority levels

Business Cost

  • Senior designers spent disproportionate time on re-work and explanation
  • Platform pattern drift accelerated as new designers made decisions without precedent
  • Cross-functional alignment workshops failed without shared design vocabulary
  • Design quality was a function of team, not org — unpredictable at scale

"I didn't know what 'platform thinking' meant until six months in. I was making product decisions when I should have been making system decisions."

— Mid-level designer, retrospective feedback

Why This Matters for Netflix's Developer Platform

Platform design organizations are uniquely demanding for designers because the users are technical, the systems are complex, and the decisions have outsized leverage. A pattern decision made on one surface propagates across a whole platform. A designer who doesn't understand system-level thinking can create beautiful screens that undermine platform coherence.

This program was explicitly about building platform-native designers — people who think first about how a decision fits the system, then about how it serves the user, rather than treating those as separate concerns. That capability is the core of what makes a developer platform design team effective at scale.

The MCP-integrated AI copilot also reflects a deeper literacy in the software engineering workflows that engineers live in daily — from Git-based configuration and CLI interactions to CI/CD pipelines. Designing tooling that integrates into those workflows, rather than beside them, is what turns a useful product into a genuinely adopted one.

The Program Architecture: Four Pillars

1
Structured Onboarding & Systems Immersion
I designed a structured onboarding program for new designers that covered platform architecture, RDS component contracts, UPX interaction standards, the Design Forum process, and decision precedents — in roughly two weeks instead of two months. Included an interactive system primer, annotated real designs with rationale, and documented "why does this pattern exist" context that was previously only held in senior designers' heads.
2
1:1 Mentorship & Individual Growth Plans
I maintained an active mentorship practice with designers across levels, focused specifically on systems thinking, platform-level problem framing, and case study development. Each relationship had a co-created growth plan with measurable milestones, regular feedback loops, and explicit skill mapping. The goal wasn't just to improve individual work — it was to grow designers who could independently navigate platform complexity.
3
Cross-Functional Design Thinking Workshops
I built and facilitated a scalable workshop toolkit for PMs and engineers, creating shared design vocabulary across disciplines. These sessions covered problem framing, user journey mapping, design critique frameworks, and how to engage productively in design reviews. Cross-functional teams that went through the program showed measurably more productive design discussions and fewer late-cycle UX revisions.
4
AI-Assisted Design Education Tooling
I designed and prototyped an AI-powered learning copilot that ingested RDS component metadata via MCP and provided contextual guidance during design work. Designers could query the system in natural language: "What pattern should I use for this filter scenario?" "What are the accessibility requirements for this modal?" The copilot reduced reliance on senior designer availability as the gating factor for standards compliance.

The AI Education Copilot in Detail

The most technically novel part of the enablement work was the AI-assisted learning tool, developed before AI copilots were standard practice in design workflows.

How It Worked

I mapped RDS component metadata — usage guidelines, behavioral specs, accessibility requirements, design rationale — into a structured, machine-readable format. Working with the AI engineering team, this was ingested via MCP and made available as a conversational interface for designers. Rather than searching documentation or asking a colleague, designers could ask contextual questions during active design work and receive standards-compliant answers instantly.

What It Enabled

  • Asynchronous standards guidance: Designers in different time zones could get accurate system guidance without waiting for a senior colleague to be available
  • Reduced drift during exploration: Designers exploring new patterns could check compliance in real time rather than discovering misalignment in review
  • Onboarding acceleration: New designers could ask "why does this pattern exist?" and get the documented rationale, rather than depending on oral knowledge transfer
  • Senior designer leverage: Senior designers spent less time answering repeat questions about component usage, focusing their attention on novel problems

This work was an early exploration of what is now called "design system AI copilots." Building it required me to think carefully about the same AI UX problems I formalized in the AI Experience Standard: how do you present AI-generated guidance in a way that's transparent about its basis, appropriate about its confidence, and designed not to replace judgment but to support it?

The Tradeoff We Accepted

What We Accepted

I chose to invest in structured enablement infrastructure (documented onboarding, workshop toolkits, mentorship frameworks) at the cost of some personal design delivery bandwidth. Building programs takes time that isn't producing Figma files.

Why

At a certain scale, the leverage of one senior designer's direct output is lower than the leverage of that designer improving the output quality of 30. The math changes when you're working on a platform with six product teams. Systems-level enablement compounds: a designer who learns to think in platform terms improves every decision they make for the rest of their career at the company. That ROI dwarfs individual feature delivery.
